A/B Eyetracking

Eye see what you're looking at: Quantifying gaze and attention

Often times attention, gaze, and perception are used interchangeably. While they are interconnected, these cognitive processes reveal different things about how a person is engaging with the world. Many factors affect one's attention, which is often guided by gaze, and thus one's perception and action.

For one of our projects in CS1300: User Interfaces + User Experience, I, along with 3 students at the Rhode Island School of Design wanted to see what kind of design tactics would attract the most interest and focus from users at a neural level. Using HerokuApp, we launched two different Taxi sites with varying visual elements.

This project was done using Sketch, HTML/CSS, HerokuApp, and the Tobii Pro X3-120 Eyetracker.

Statistics

Click Through Rate

Proportion of sessions that had at least 1 click

Calculated by taking the number of unique clicks and dividing that by the number of unique sessions to get the proportion of sessions that had at least one click. Only the user’s first interaction was included in the calculation.

Return Rate

Proportion of sessions that left the landing page and returned

Calculated using the number of returns divided by the number of clicks to evaluate the proportion of sessions that left the landing page and returned. These are presented as percentages.

Dwell time

Average time users spent on linked pages before returning to the landing page

Calculated by Time to Click (Time it took for a user to click our website) - Time it took to load the 2nd page

Eyetracker

We used a Tobii Pro X3-120, a screen-based eye tracker that captures gaze data at 120 Hz, to capture our gaze data.

We hypothesized that site Version A would yield longer eye static towards the buttons and draw attention towards pressing the buttons compared to site Version B.

We noticed that, for site Version B, the user’s eyes don’t linger anywhere; rather, the user seems very goal and path oriented.

Version A yielded more goal and path-oriented results and Version B yielded more leisurely interest in the layout of the site.

Findings

Version A or B?

We would recommend the design of Version A if one wants one's customer base to be bewildered and intrigued by the flashy, flamboyant, not-so-prettily designed layout.

The eye-catching yellow color, which hints at a Taxi service’s traditional black and white color scheme, may encourage the users to book using Version A.

However, we would further recommend cleaning up and organizing the layout so that the site is at once captivating, but also informative and professional.

Although statistically, the results showed that there was no significant difference between Version A and Version B, if one takes into consideration the qualitative results that were gathered from the eye-tracking test, we would suggest improving upon Version A (though we would suggest analyzing more data, as we had insufficient data, which we believe affected our results).
